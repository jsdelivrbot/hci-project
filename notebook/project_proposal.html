Observations

Family eating dinner

Three people sit at the dinner table to eat at the end of the day. While eating, everyone is using their smartphone — they are looking downwards toward their smartphone screen. One person has earphones on while watching a YouTube video. Another is looking at the day's e-mails and planifying their day for tomorrow. The third is reading articles. When asked questions, short and somewhat dismissive answers are given. Sometimes a person interrupts what they are doing to read and answer to a new message (Facebook, Snapchat, SMS). There is no conversation going on at the table, only occasional short questions and answers.

Significant others in the living room

Two people are in the living room. Netflix is running, the dog is sleeping and humans are silent. One is watching Netflix while the other is reading some articles. Both subjects have their phone nearby ready for any alert. The subject reading article (Female) is often interrupted by the multiple group chats that are going on on multiple platforms (Messenger, SMS, Snapchat), those group chats are mostly for social purpose (not work or school related). The other one (Male) is mostly getting emails related to work and answers them right away. Once the individual activities are completed and they get into a conversation together, the same scenario arises: as soon as they get an alert, they reply. The main difference is that after the reply, they don't stick to their smartphone to pursue other activities, they join back in the conversation.

Roommates hanging out

Three roommates are sitting in the communal area of their apartment. They chose to sit here for company, but instantly pull out their cell phones. The individuals look away from their phone at times, but usually glance back fairly quickly. An individual putting down his phone does not trigger the others to do so. On the other hand, when one of them speaks up, they all stop using their phones and listen. Oral conversations seem to have precedence on digital ones.

The Problem

As we observed, people tend to spend a great amount of time using their smartphones for socialization. Activities are varied: texting friends through SMS or iMessage, checking a Facebook feed, organizing a work-related e-mail inbox, or sending ephemeral messages through Snapchat. Even when in groups, with friends or family, while eating or just hanging out, people tend to confine themselves to their smartphones. This can be seen in the picture below, taken during the last observation session.

Our observations suggest that this is due to the hard-to-resist pull of sheer information and total entertainment. This is in line with a recent study from the McCombs School of Business at The University of Texas at Austin, which reveals that the presence of person's smartphone nearby is enough to distract them and "reduce available cognitive capacity" [1].

The convenience of social interaction through a smartphone is also hard to match. Sending a message is quick and easy, compared to the effort required by a telephone call or a meeting in person. The entertainment value of the message can also be augmented by including a funny picture, a video, or a series of emojis -- things that aren't available to rely on in conventional face-to-face interaction. A side-effect of the greatly facilitated communication brought by technology is that messages tend to be uniform and direct. Laughter is reduced to a few characters and jokes have trendy and easily-replicable formats. Many conversations are also results-focused -- for example, agreeing on quick meet-ups, or dry Slack chats.

With this in mind, our goal is to add some variety to social interaction and encourage face-to-face conversation. Effectively, we want to create a smartphone application that deters users from excessive useof their smartphone for socialization. However, applications to prevent direct smartphone use already exist.Rather, we want to take a fun-centered approach. To do this, the application would suggest an uncommon word, a phrase, a question, a discussion topic, or other such facilitators to use in real-life conversation.

Personas

Our observations relate specifically to smartphone users in social environments, since we would like to reduce smartphone use in these situations. We grouped these individuals into two personas: those receptive to face-to-face social interaction, and those unreceptive to it. We noted that the environment in which a person is will influence their receptivity: a person in a group of friends and acquaintances will be more receptive to their attempts at socializing, whereas a person surrounded by strangers will be more likely to isolate themselves.

The Receptive Individual

Even if this person is using their smartphone in a social environment, they will be open to further face-to-face interaction. They may be reading and replying to messages, or interacting with other information that requires little attention. They will be ready to quit using their smartphone if there is a conversation going on, but may revert to it if there is a lull. This person’s smartphone is used to satisfy boredom. They may or may not try to initiate conversations (depending on the degree to which they are hooked to their phone), but will be eager to participate in one. These users would be ready to accept a word-based challenge or a discussion topic.

The Unreceptive Individual

This person is absorbed by what they are doing on their phone. The content they are interacting with requires more attention: a video, a mobile game, or a quick social feed. They may also be using earphones for videos or music, further isolating them from their surroundings. When asked questions, they will appear bothered, due to being dragged away from the smartphone that requires their attention. If alone, they may benefit from icebreaker challenges. If in a group of friends, they could be dragged into a lively conversation that people who are more receptive are engaged in.

Use Case Scenario

To illustrate the use of our application, let's take the case of the roommates above. They all know each other well, but may find that using their smartphone is easier than sparking a conversation. In this case, one person could open the application and quickly find a suggested question, or a word, or a similar challenge that could facilitate social interaction. The person could also have used the application earlier in the day, and kept the suggestion in the back of their mind. Say the application suggests a funny word. The user would try to think of a way to steer conversation towards using this word. Roommates that are receptive would react well to this subject introduction and let their phones rest; others that are less so may be pulled in if the conversation gets lively. Afterwards, if the suggestion was used, the user would revisit the application to mark as a success the introduction of the app suggestion. This would be rewarded in some way: a point system could be implemented, or a picture shown (social media share buttons would go against the purpose of the application, so we opt not to use them).

For this scenario to be a success, the application would have to be simple, efficient, and most of all engaging. The user's interaction with the application would have to be very low-friction, in the sense that the aim is to provide a small snippet of information and simple interactions in a straightforward, not cumbersome, manner. Most of all, the design should reflect the whimsical, but ultimately little, challenge that is presented. It should be an exercise in restraint and character, and should require very little learning time.

Related Products

Mobile phones have access to two main types of external content: mobile applications and web pages. Products resembling our design are found on the Internet or on the phone’s respective digital distribution platform (iOS App Store or Google Play).

Web based products

Several websites exist with the main purpose of distributing a “word of the day”. The main notification system that is used is an email subscription service. WordThink provides a word of the day that is culturally significant. The words are taken from news articles and try not to be obscure or too pretentious. The word of the day and its definition can be viewed on the website’s front page or in the daily email notification. There is no example usage of the word or any information about the word’s etymology. The website styling is archaic and does not follow modern trends. Moreover, there is currently a link to a mobile application, but the link is invalid. Users must therefore check their emails or access the web page to view the provided new word.

Wordsmith also provides a word of the day on its front page, but aims at ameliorating their users’ vocabulary. The words are fairly obscure and tend to be for experienced English speakers. Pronunciation, etymology and a usage example are also provided. Furthermore, the website’s main goal is to provide its online community interesting content related to linguistics. The website also offers other services, such as an anagram maker, a chat and a news site for anagrams. The main notification service is once again an email subscription service. The styling is archaic, but very simple and direct. The image below is a screen grab of the webpage.

Mobile applications

The Word of the Day! by Silvia Martinez and WordOfTheDay by Unity are mobile apps available on the iOS and Android app stores providing a mobile equivalent of the websites described above. Only the word’s definition is provided and a history of the past words seen by the user. The apps are directed towards English speakers wanting to enhance their vocabulary. The apps styling is simple and modern, providing very clear access to the app’s content.

Product Comparison

There are currently no products that perfectly satisfy our users’ needs. The applications and websites described in the previous section focus on providing the content, and not on interacting with the user. They are educational sources, in a sense, as they teach the user a new word. Our product’s goal is to encourage face-to-face conversations and lessen the strain of first encounters by providing icebreakers. It also records your progress by providing a tracking system and presents small rewards when a word or phrase is used during the day, such as tokens, badges and points. The proposed design is also more appealing to our target user base because of its modern styling and reward-based interactions. Social media provides direct feedback for most actions taken by users through the use of digital points (likes on Facebook, or karma on Reddit), and is heavily used by people in the 10-50 age group. Our notification system will alert users and remind them to interact with their surroundings; it does not require that users remember to check their emails or visit a specific web page. The target audience of our design is well accustomed to being notified on their smartphones and responding to these notifications. This is why we have chosen a mobile application with notifications and a reward-based interaction system.

High-Level Design

The scope of the project is solely based on software development and design. Any hardware used is already existant and will be required to run the application (smartphone) and its infrastructure (server).

Custom Components

Mobile app: The mobile application will be cross-platform (Android and iOS). It will be created using existant frameworks such as ionic, nativescript or meteor, which allow for Web-based development of cross-platform applications.
Local Database: The local database will be used to store local data, such as word usage history or words for offline usage.
Push Notification Service: The push notification service will be used to notify a user about his "Daily Word Suggestion", or another such feature.

Existing Components

Word Definition API: This external API will be used to retrieve external words, their definition and other specifications, such as frequency of use and familiarity.
Text to Voice API: This external API will be used so that the user can hear the pronunciation of the proposed word.

Optional Components

DB Server: The database server can be used for multiple purpose and advanced functionalities. For example, to gather data regarding word usage, application usage, etc.
REST API: This API will be custom made using existing frameworks such as the Django REST Framework. It will be used to have access to data stored in the database over the Internet.

In order to ensure the project feasibility, we have chosen a set of technologies that matches the variety of skills in our team and where every team member will be important to bring the project to an end.

The mobile app development will be ensured by the skills of all team members combined, which already have experience in both Android and iOS. Also, in order to reduce development time and offer an app for both iOS and Android users, an existing cross-platform framework will be leveraged instead of developing two separate applications. This framework is based on web technologies, such as Javascript, HTML, and CSS, which can be easily used by our team.

The local database portion of it can be easily managed by one of the team member by using a simple file based, embedded database.

The push notification service will be implemented using the tools built-in the cross-platform framework.

The integration with external services will be made simple, because the response is provided in JSON format, by doing a GET request. Again, any of our team members with web skills will be able to perform this.

Also, the cloud component (Database server and REST API on top of it) has been made optional, because they are not necessary for the application to be running and tested by a human subject. Once we have a first working prototype, we will re-evaluate if it's possible to add it in. Based on the significant skills of the team in SQL, database and API design, implementing this could be challenging, but could be executed with proper time management.

To ensure proper tracking of all items to be completed and their assignments, the GitHub Projects feature will be used. This will allow all developers to be on track and optimize efforts based on our individual skills.

Finally, the main strategy to make this project feasible is to use everyone's skill effectively by using existing and known technologies that accelerate development and by doing proper task management.
